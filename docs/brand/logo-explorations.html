Reading each candidate honestly

A (Newsprint heritage): the bravest pick. Carries the deepest cycling story (L'Auto → maillot jaune is unforgettable once explained). Tone-of-voice match is high: founder-led, considered, has a reason behind every visual choice. Forces a brand evolution: yellow becomes canvas, navy becomes accent. If you want Cycling Zone to feel like a brand made by someone who actually loves cycling history, this is it. The risk is technical (yellow accents need a navy alternative), not aesthetic.

B (Velodrome wood): the warm-athletic compromise. Less brand-story than A but still has cycling-DNA (track racing). Yellow accent stays usable. Pas Normal Studios meets considered SaaS. Feels "premium without being precious". Strong if the brand should lean "athletic" more than "media/editorial".

C (Race-bib paper): the practical pick. Most flexible long-term, most data-dense-friendly. Cycling-DNA is the most subtle (you have to look for it). Honest, modern, won't fight Phase 4 UI when surface complexity grows. If A and D feel "too much", this is the safety net that still beats generic.

D (Cobblestone fog): the contrarian pick. Maximum differentiation from every other cycling app and SaaS. Gritty premium, unmistakably considered. Tone-of-voice match: highest, feels like a founder picked this specifically, not a template. Risk: gray-leaning apps can feel low-energy; mitigation depends on Phase 4 UI carrying strong yellow accents.

My honest take: if you want to go full-narrative and tell a real story with the brand, A. If you want differentiation without committing to yellow-as-canvas, D. If you want safety-with-character, B. Drop C only if cycling-DNA matters more than data-density to you, but it's also the lowest-risk pick. The "right" answer depends on whether you want the brand to be a story or a stage.
